How to Read a PM’s Book

by V. Dimopoulos · Research note #4 · field notes on factor dissection and the drawdown protocol · July 17, 2026

A portfolio is a set of claims. The portfolio manager claims the returns come from his ideas; the risk seat’s job is to find out what the book actually owns. These are field notes from fifteen years on the risk side of that conversation — two disciplines, both practiced nightly by this museum’s own machine on its own strategies.

Part I — The dissection: intended versus unintended

The organizing question is never “is the book risky?” — risk is the job. The question is which exposures were chosen, and which arrived uninvited. A fundamental stock picker is paid for idiosyncratic judgment; everything else in the book is either deliberate ballast or a stowaway. The dissection proceeds in six layers, cheapest explanation first:

  1. Market. Net and gross beta. The oldest stowaway: a “hedged” book whose longs are high-beta and shorts low-beta is net long the market while its net dollars say zero.
  2. Styles. Size, value, momentum, quality, low-volatility, short-term reversal. Regress the book’s returns on the factor set; check loadings against what the PM thinks he runs.
  3. Industry and sector. Concentrations, and the peer-relative view — a “diversified” book of one sector’s suppliers is one bet in many costumes.
  4. Macro sensitivities. Rates, oil, the dollar — whatever the book’s names transmit. Duration is not only a bond concept.
  5. Crowding and liquidity. Ownership overlap with other funds; days-to-liquidate at a sane participation rate. The position that cannot be exited is the one that decides the worst week.
  6. The residual share — the punchline. After all of the above: what fraction of the book’s risk is actually idiosyncratic? A “stock picker” whose variance is mostly factor is a closet factor fund at stock-picker fees. And run it rolling, not quarterly: weights drift, and books wander into factor bets that nobody chose — passivity is not factor-neutrality.

Two humility clauses from this museum’s own workshop. First, the blame counter: dollar weights lie about where risk lives — decompose by risk contribution, because the pieces sum exactly and the ranking rarely matches the position sheet. Second, from our random-matrix work: even the “residuals” can hide a common factor in a concentrated regime — idiosyncratic is a label to be verified, not assumed.

Part II — The drawdown protocol: when the PM is losing money

The worst time to design a policy is inside the loss. The protocol exists so that nobody has to be brave or brilliant on the worst day — only obedient to decisions made on a calm one.

  1. Diagnose before acting. Decompose the loss: factor or idiosyncratic? A few names or the whole book? Inside the risk budget or breaching it? “Is he losing on his thesis, or on exposures he didn’t know he had?” — the two have opposite remedies, and acting before answering treats both wrongly.
  2. Run the pre-committed ladder. Early-warning indicator → the PM adjusts; first threshold → escalation; second → mandatory de-risking. Mechanical de-grossing at drawdown levels is not a failure of nerve — it is the nerve, exercised in advance. Triggers set at promotion are never renegotiated mid-drawdown; this museum applies the same rule to its own strategies.
  3. Cut in the right order. Gross first — it lowers the dose while preserving the PM’s relative bets. Unintended factor exposures second — hedge or trim the stowaways; they were never the thesis. Concentrated and illiquid positions early — the exit you cannot make later must be right-sized while you still can. The highest-conviction idiosyncratic names last: they are the reason the PM has a seat.
  4. Separate loss from error. Good process loses money on bad days; that is what risk means. The review asks whether the process was followed, not whether the outcome was pleasant. But repeated breaches are structure, not weather — and structure escalates.
  5. Talk to the PM. Thesis intact, or broken by new information? The risk seat’s deepest job is keeping a skilled PM solvent long enough for the skill to compound — and knowing the difference between defending a position and defending a person.
Why this hangs in the museum: the machine behind this site runs both disciplines on itself — risk-contribution blame counters and liquidity slices on every backtest, and pre-committed retirement triggers on everything promoted. It retired the founder’s own namesake model under those triggers. A protocol you exempt yourself from is a brochure.
