Measuring the Crowd

by V. Dimopoulos · Research note #6 · the 13F crowding study: two real effects, one held bar · July 23, 2026

The Quant Quake plaque in the Case Studies wing ends with a promise: crowding is a factor to be measured, not a surprise to be survived — and a study sits in the museum’s own queue. This note keeps that promise. The study was pre-registered before a single filing was downloaded: one crowding measure, two claims, the hedge-fund filter defined in advance, the family of tests declared at n = 2 so the multiplicity deflator knew what was coming.

The data

The SEC publishes every institutional holdings report (Form 13F) as structured data. I took 53 quarters of it — 2013 through mid-2026, 2.9 GB — and identified, among 11,750 filers, the 1,216 that behave like hedge funds: fewer than 250 positions and quarterly name-turnover above 25%, both thresholds written down before any results existed. A stock’s crowding in a given quarter is simply how many of those funds hold it — usable only after the 45-day filing deadline, because the museum does not read tomorrow’s newspaper.

Claim one: the crowd moves together

If many funds hold the same two stocks, those stocks should co-move beyond what sector, size, and the six standard factors explain — the classic “connected stocks” result. They do: across 3,312 co-held pairs, each matched against a control of the same sector and size that the crowd does not co-hold, residual correlation runs higher by about 0.9 correlation points, with a Newey-West t-statistic of 4.09. Real, and small.

Claim two: the crowd bleeds together

The Quant Quake signature: in bad months, crowded names should underperform uncrowded ones, because one fund’s deleveraging is every co-holder’s drawdown. In the worst decile of market months since 2014, the most-crowded quintile of my panel underperformed the least-crowded by 142 basis points per month, factor-adjusted (t = 3.9). Across all months the spread is −33 basis points — the crowd pays a small toll in ordinary times and a heavy one in storms, which is exactly what the 2007 story predicts.

The verdict, and why it is the point

Both effects are statistically significant. Neither was promoted. The museum’s bar demands that a claim survive its margin-of-safety rerun — roughly, a raw t-statistic of 5 — and 4.09 and 3.9 do not. Fourteen stress months is precisely the small sample the pre-registration predicted, and a small sample is a small sample no matter how well it agrees with your thesis. So the finding stands as the pre-registration said it must: crowding is a risk measure, not a signal. It tells a risk desk where the exits are narrow; it does not tell a trading desk what to buy.

Declared limits

Three, written before and during — never after. The panel is survivorship-biased (current constituents only), so crowding is counted among survivors. The pre-registration specified industry matching; my data honestly supports sector matching, and the deviation was recorded in the code before results existed. And 13F filings see no shorts and no swaps — by construction, an Archegos is invisible to this measure, which is not a footnote but a lesson: the crowding you can measure is the floor, not the ceiling.

Why this hangs in the museum: most shops would have shipped these two t-statistics as product. The museum’s scoreboard pays honesty and taxes theater — significant-but-not- promoted is a category, and it is the category that keeps the two signals that did survive worth believing. The bar that refused this study is the same bar that validates everything it ever passes.
