The Propulsion Room

Flight mechanics for portfolios · thrust, drag, gravity, and the price of steering · July 2026

An aircraft is a negotiation between four forces. So is a portfolio. This room translates the museum’s own telemetry — the strategies that flew far enough to be measured — into the engineer’s vocabulary, because the engineer’s vocabulary is honest: nothing flies on thrust alone, and most designs die of drag, not of weak engines.

The measured friction ratios

Of the strategies with full flight telemetry, only about a third produced positive thrust at all. Among those that did, the median friction ratio — cost drag as a share of gross thrust — was 42%: nearly half of everything the engine produced was burned overcoming drag before a dollar reached the account. The median stall speed — the cost level at which net return hits zero — was about 12 bps per unit of turnover. Our model assumes a generous 5; real institutional all-in costs for anything less liquid run 10–20. A strategy whose breakeven is 12 bps is not a flying machine; it is a machine that flies in a wind tunnel.

Steering outcomes under load

Steering is not free: each course correction (rebalance) pays the friction toll, so steering authority must be budgeted like fuel. The telemetry median here is ~10× annual turnover; the worst steerers hit ~19×. The gate that matters is the signal half-life check: a pilot who corrects course faster than the instruments update is not steering, he is oscillating — and a strategy whose signal half-life is shorter than its rebalance period pays turnover to chase noise. That check is stage-4 standard here.

Gravity loads

Volatility drag is the gravity of compounding: geometric growth ≈ arithmetic return minus half the variance. One measured strategy in this collection flew arithmetically — positive average return — and still sank geometrically, because its variance load exceeded its lift. This is how an exhibit with a positive “average” turns \$100k into \$93k over twenty years. Leverage multiplies thrust and gravity alike; LTCM’s plaque in the Masterpieces Gallery is what a structural failure under g-load looks like.

The glider and the fighter jet

The index fund is a glider: almost no thrust of its own, almost no drag (single-digit bps, near-zero turnover), and it converts gravity into forward motion — compounding does the flying. Lift-to-drag ratio: enormous. Most active strategies are fighter jets: impressive thrust, violent maneuvers, and a fuel burn that demands the mission be worth it. The museum’s standing finding is that most jets tested here could not outrun the glider after paying for their own fuel. The bar for flying anything active is therefore precise: thrust minus drag minus gravity must beat the glider — net, at realistic friction, measured out of sample.

Telemetry caveat, stated plainly: full flight data exists for the 11 strategies that reached the backtest stage with complete cost accounting; research-lane studies and external sims track different instruments. Small sample, honest numbers.
