620 Probabilities

by V. Dimopoulos · Research note #5 · what a World Cup forecasting competition taught the museum about calibration · July 20, 2026

This summer, Jump Trading ran a public forecasting competition on the 2026 World Cup — the Probability Cup. Every entrant submits probabilities on match questions (will there be 22+ shots? will Spain put 5 on target?), and every forecast is scored by the Brier score: the squared error between your stated probability and what happened. There is no arguing with it, no narrative, no partial credit for a good story. It is the cleanest scoreboard in the forecasting business, and it is the reason this museum entered a bot: we wanted our research habits graded by a referee we couldn’t charm.

Final standing: top 6% of entrants. Here is the part worth writing about — the bot got there with no hero calls at all.

The arithmetic of honesty

The Brier score of always answering 50% is 0.25 — the coin-flip baseline. Across 620 settled forecasts, the bot’s mean Brier was 0.2309. That is a roughly two-cent edge over the coin, per question. Nobody brags about two cents at a dinner party. But a proper scoring rule compounds honesty the way markets compound returns: a small real edge, taken 620 independent times, walks you quietly past the field — most of which is either guessing (0.25) or, worse, confident (much more than 0.25).

This is the same law that runs the museum’s equity pipeline — Grinold’s fundamental law, IR = IC × √breadth. Skill compounds through the number of honest, independent attempts, not the loudness of any one of them. The Medallion plaque in our Case Studies wing says it for trading; the Probability Cup says it for forecasting. It is apparently just true.

The calibration table, flaws included

Calibration asks the only question that matters for a probabilist: when you say 40%, does it happen 40% of the time? Here is the bot’s full table — including the two rows we are least proud of, because a museum that only hangs its flattering angles is a gift shop.

Read the two red rows carefully; they are opposite sins. In the middle, the bot knew more than it said: when it whispered “51%,” reality answered “60%.” Hedging toward the coin feels prudent and is actually a tax — 40% of all forecasts huddled in the 40–60 band. At the top, the opposite: the nine times the bot felt nearly certain, it was right barely half the time. Certainty was rare and still overpriced. The tail is where hubris lives, in forecasting as in portfolios — our LTCM plaque makes the identical point with nine fewer decimal places.

What transfers to markets

  1. Breadth beats heroics. Top 6% with zero memorable predictions. The museum’s pipeline is built on the same bet: 182 strategies tested, promotion decided by accumulated honest attempts, not by any single backtest’s fireworks.
  2. Get a referee you can’t charm. The Brier score is a proper scoring rule: the only way to maximize it is to report what you actually believe. Our promotion gates (Newey-West t-statistics, margin-of-safety reruns, multiple-testing deflators) are our attempt to build the same property into equity research — a scoreboard that pays honesty and taxes theater.
  3. The market is the prior. The bot’s edge came from disciplined base rates updated with public information — not from out-thinking the crowd from scratch. Prediction markets are currently being quantified by the largest firms in the world for exactly this reason: they are young markets, and young markets pay the early and the systematic.
  4. Publish your misses with your rank. The red rows above cost the bot places on the leaderboard, and they are the most useful rows in the table — next cycle’s bot sharpens the 50s and mutes the 90s. A forecast log you cannot audit is a highlight reel.
Why this hangs in the museum: the collection’s whole argument is that tested breadth under an incorruptible scoreboard beats brilliance under a flattering one. The Probability Cup was a chance to make that argument in someone else’s arena, against a live field, with a referee owned by a firm that does this for a living — and the argument held.
