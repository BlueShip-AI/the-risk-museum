What the Swarm Knows and What It Does Not Know

by V. Dimopoulos · Research note #8 · the machine’s own dark caps, published on purpose · July 23, 2026

In 1964 the Hungarian mathematician László Fejes Tóth published a paper with a perfect title: What the Bees Know and What They Do Not Know. The bees, it turns out, are optimal where you can see: the hexagonal comb is provably the cheapest way to divide a plane into equal cells — though proving it against every curved and conspiring alternative took our species until 1999. But at the bottom of each cell, where two layers of comb meet in the dark, Fejes Tóth showed the bees’ three-panel cap can be beaten by a fraction of one percent of wax. Perfect in the visible architecture. A whisker off in the dark. And no bee will ever know either fact.

The research machine behind this museum is named the swarm, so the question inherits itself: what does our swarm know, and what does it not know? Most research shops publish only the first half. This note is the second half, hung deliberately.

What the swarm knows

It knows what it has tested: 212 hypotheses to date, each through the same gates — Newey-West statistics, margin-of-safety reruns, multiplicity deflators — with every verdict, including the founder’s own retired namesake model, on the public wall. It knows the difference between significant and promoted: this month it found two real crowding effects (t of 4.1 and 3.9) and declined to promote either, because the bar is the bar. And it knows its arithmetic is not an opinion: the statistics are deterministic code; no language model in this shop is permitted to compute so much as a Sharpe ratio. When mathematicians spent four years refereeing the sphere-packing proof and could only reach “99% certain,” Thomas Hales spent a decade more getting a machine to verify every line. That exchange rate — performance is cheap, certainty is expensive — is the one this museum trades at.

What the swarm does not know

Here are our dark caps, stated as plainly as we can see them.

  1. The universe flatters us. Our panel is today’s index constituents — companies that survived. Every Sharpe ratio on the wall is an upper bound for this reason, and says so.
  2. The regimes we haven’t met. Our sample contains a few crises; the future is not obligated to draw from the same urn. Correlations measured in calm are fiction in storms — we stress for this, but a stress test is a rehearsal, not the weather.
  3. Capacity is assumed, not proven. Every backtest presumes our trading does not move the price. At our size that is nearly true, which is a statement about our size, not our cleverness.
  4. The crowding we cannot see. Our own 13F study measures crowding among long positions in public filings — no shorts, no swaps. The floor of the crowd, never its ceiling.
  5. The near-fits we will be offered. Newton was right that twelve spheres kiss and a thirteenth never quite fits, though the slack looks so inviting — and proving it took 250 years. Every week our pipeline meets a strategy at t = 2.3 that looks like it almost fits. The slack is real. It still doesn’t fit.

The bee and the mathematician

The bee only has to be right; the mathematician has to be certain — certain against every competitor, including the infinitely many nobody has drawn. A backtest that works is the bee: honestly impressive, locally optimal, blind to its own caps. A claim that survives pre-registration, stress, and deflation is a step toward the mathematician’s standard — never all the way there, because markets are not geometry. The museum’s wager is that in a market full of confident bees, the durable edge belongs to whoever keeps honest books about the dark.

Why this hangs in the museum: a research process that cannot name what it doesn’t know will eventually sell you what it doesn’t have. The caps above are permanent exhibits. When we find another, it goes on this wall — that is the whole policy, and Fejes Tóth said it first: the bees are not perfect. They are merely astonishing, and only a whisker off in the dark.
