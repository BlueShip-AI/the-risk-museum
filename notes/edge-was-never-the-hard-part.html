The Edge Was Never the Hard Part

by V. Dimopoulos · Research note #7 · the biased-coin experiment, the Kelly criterion, and how the museum sizes · July 23, 2026

In 2016, two researchers handed sixty-one finance-trained people a coin that lands heads 60% of the time, thirty minutes, twenty-five dollars, and a simple instruction: bet on flips, keep what you make (capped at $250). The game is guaranteed to favor the player. It is difficult to design a friendlier casino.

Twenty-eight percent of them went completely broke. About a third finished below their starting stake. Only one in five reached the cap that sensible play makes nearly automatic. The average payout was $91 — on a machine engineered to print money. The study is Haghani and Dewey, Observed Betting Patterns on a Biased Coin, and it deserves a plaque because of what it isolates: these players had the edge handed to them, gift-wrapped, with the probability printed on the box. The thing that ruined them was the one variable left in their control — how much to bet.

The curve that decides everything

The answer they needed dates to 1956, when John Kelly at Bell Labs noticed that a gambler with an edge is a communication channel with signal in it. For an even-money bet, the growth-optimal fraction of your bankroll is twice your win probability minus one: for a 60/40 coin, bet 20%. Plot long-run growth against bet size and you get a hill: zero at zero (bet nothing, make nothing), peaking at 20% (about 2% compounded per flip), falling past it — and crossing zero near 39%. Bet 40% of your bankroll on this guaranteed- winning coin, every flip, and your wealth shrinks at about a quarter percent per flip. Same edge. Wrong size. Slow ruin.

The hill hides a second lesson in its asymmetry. Bet half of Kelly — 10% instead of 20% — and you keep roughly three-quarters of the maximum growth rate for about half the swings. Underbetting costs a little; overbetting costs everything. When in doubt, miss low. And the expected-value crowd should sit down for this one: over 300 flips at full Kelly, the average outcome on a $25 start is over $3 million — and the median is about $10,000. You do not get to live in the average of all universes. You live in one, and the log of your wealth — not the mean of your fantasies — is what compounds there. That gap is volatility drag, the same quiet thief on the Bernoulli plaque in our Antiquities Wing, the reason exhibits with positive average returns still turned $100k into $93k.

Why this hangs in the museum

Three house rules descend from that hill.

  1. Kelly is brutally sensitive to your edge estimate — overrate the edge and the formula marches you past the peak into the downslope. So the museum’s sizing doctrine starts one step earlier: the edge that enters any sizing arithmetic is the stressed estimate — gross halved, costs doubled — never the raw backtest. A margin of safety on the input is worth more than precision in the formula.
  2. The lopsided hill is why the gates never loosen. A fake survivor promoted by a loose gate doesn’t just waste capital; it invites Kelly-sized bets on an edge of zero, which the curve says is negative growth dressed as opportunity.
  3. Survival is the strategy. Multiplication is unforgiving: a single zero anywhere in the chain of returns collapses the product. Every drawdown rule in this museum — and the pre-committed ladders in our drawdown-protocol note — exists because there is no comeback from nothing, no matter how good the edge that remains.

One more thing, offered with respect rather than irony: one of the experiment’s authors was a founding partner of Long-Term Capital Management, whose plaque hangs in our Blowup Wing for the canonical sizing catastrophe. The man who lived the hardest version of this lesson went on to design the cleanest demonstration of it. That is what turning a loss into a curriculum looks like, and the museum takes the example seriously.

The one-sentence version, for the wall: a positive edge is a necessary condition; surviving your own bet size is the sufficient one. The edge was never the hard part.
